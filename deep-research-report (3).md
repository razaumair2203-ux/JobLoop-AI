## The scorecard that is strong enough to run the loop

The most robust design is a **hierarchical scorecard** with **hard gates**, **subscores**, **reason codes**, and **uncertainty**, rather than a naked 0ÔÇô100 number. I recommend four top-level outputs for every candidate/JD pair.

**Eligibility status** should be a gate, not a weighted feature. ## Conflict resolution, enrichment, and the main CV anchor points

The cleanest internal representation is a **candidate knowledge graph** or canonical profile made of claims, not documents. Each uploaded CV becomes a source of claims about jobs, dates, titles, skills, projects, education, certifications, locations, languages, and links. Each claim should store provenance: source file, extraction timestamp, parser confidence, normalised taxonomy mapping, and attached evidence. This turns ÔÇťmultiple CV uploadÔÇŁ from a document problem into a record-fusion problem. Parser vendors such as `ţłÇurlţłéAffindaţłéturn23search2ţłü`, `ţłÇurlţłéRChilliţłéturn22view1ţłü`, and `ţłÇurlţłéTextkernel Skills Intelligenceţłéturn22view3ţłü` already expose parsing, job parsing, matching, taxonomy, or skill-extraction primitives that fit naturally into this architecture. ţłÇciteţłéturn23search2ţłéturn23search1ţłéturn22view0ţłéturn22view1ţłéturn22view2ţłéturn22view3ţłéturn22view4ţłü

Conflicts should be split into **hard conflicts** and **soft conflicts**. Hard conflicts include materially different employment dates, overlapping full-time roles that look impossible, different degrees claimed for the same year, contradictory seniority, or unverifiable mandatory credentials. Soft conflicts include title variants, company renames, abbreviations, slight date granularity differences, and wording differences that map to the same underlying fact. Hard conflicts should never be silently ÔÇťaveraged awayÔÇŁ; they should create a hold state and trigger a targeted user interaction. Soft conflicts can often be auto-resolved using taxonomy mapping, recency, or supporting evidence. The logic here mirrors what research on data fusion calls conflict resolution across multiple sources. ţłÇciteţłéturn27search4ţłéturn27search19ţłü

Your enrichment engine should operate on the **anchor points** that make a CV hireable. The official guidance from the ţłÇentityţłé["organization","National Careers Service","United Kingdom careers guidance service"]ţłü and the ţłÇentityţłé["organization","Harvard FAS Mignone Center for Career Success","career services office"]ţłü points to the main anchors: contact details, introduction/summary, education history, work history, and concise evidence of what the person actually did and achieved. For work history, enrichment questions should reliably force STAR-like detail: what was the problem, what action did you take, what tools or methods did you use, what measurable result changed, and what proof can you provide? The moment you ask ÔÇťwhat metric moved?ÔÇŁ and ÔÇťwhat can substantiate it?ÔÇŁ, you stop being a fancy text generator and start becoming a serious profile system. ţłÇciteţłéturn33view0ţłéturn33view1ţłü

## Where the human stays in the loop

The human should remain in the loop at precisely the places where risk, ambiguity, or user agency matter most. First, the user should approve the **canonical merged profile** after the first multi-CV ingestion. Second, any **hard conflict** should require a human answer or evidence upload. Third, any score that materially depends on a protected-risk-sensitive inference or unresolved ambiguity should route to review. Fourth, before submission, the user should approve the **final job-targeted CV** variant and see exactly what changed and why. Fifth, if you ever move from candidate-coaching into employer-side ranking or filtering, you will need formal review, notices, and appeals processes. That kind of meaningful human involvement is directly in line with the ICOÔÇÖs findings and the U.S. DOLÔÇÖs inclusive-hiring framing. ţłÇciteţłéturn37view0ţłéturn37view1ţłéturn19view0ţłéturn9view2ţłü
